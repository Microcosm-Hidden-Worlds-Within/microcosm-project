Clostridium difficile, commonly referred to as C. difficile, is a gram-positive, spore-forming, anaerobic bacterium. It is a significant pathogen in healthcare settings, primarily causing antibiotic-associated diarrhea and colitis. The organism's colonies exhibit distinctive morphological and biochemical characteristics that aid in its identification and differentiation from other bacterial species.

Colony Morphology

Colony Shape and Size: C. difficile colonies are typically round and have a diameter ranging from 2 to 5 millimeters after 48 hours of incubation. The colonies may appear slightly irregular and can exhibit a spread or flat appearance depending on the strain and growth conditions.

Colony Surface: The surface of C. difficile colonies is usually smooth, but can sometimes appear rough. The texture is often described as slightly granular or powdery, which is attributed to the formation of spores on the colony surface.

Color and Pigmentation: C. difficile colonies are generally white to grayish in color. They lack any distinct pigmentation, although some strains may produce slight yellow or greenish hues under certain growth conditions.

Growth Characteristics

Growth Media: C. difficile is typically cultured on selective media such as cycloserine-cefoxitin-fructose agar (CCFA) or chromogenic agar, which inhibit the growth of competing flora while allowing the selective isolation of C. difficile. The colonies on CCFA appear yellow due to the fermentation of fructose, which is a characteristic feature aiding in the identification of this bacterium.

Incubation Conditions: As an anaerobic bacterium, C. difficile requires an oxygen-free environment for optimal growth. Incubation is usually performed at 35-37°C in an anaerobic chamber or jar. Colony formation is typically observed within 24-48 hours of incubation.

Microscopic Characteristics

Gram Staining: Under the microscope, C. difficile appears as gram-positive rods. They may exhibit a characteristic “drumstick” appearance due to the presence of terminal spores, which bulge out from the end of the cells.

Spores: The spores of C. difficile are highly resistant to environmental conditions and can survive for prolonged periods outside the host. These spores are a key factor in the transmission and persistence of C. difficile infections, particularly in healthcare environments.

Biochemical Identification

Fermentation and Enzymatic Activity: C. difficile can ferment carbohydrates, producing characteristic volatile fatty acids. It is positive for fructose fermentation, which can be detected on selective media like CCFA. Additionally, C. difficile is known to produce toxins, specifically toxin A (enterotoxin) and toxin B (cytotoxin), which are critical virulence factors.

Antibiotic Resistance: C. difficile exhibits resistance to several antibiotics, including clindamycin and cephalosporins. This resistance is a key factor in the overgrowth of C. difficile in the gut following antibiotic therapy, leading to infection.

Clinical Significance

Pathogenicity: C. difficile is the leading cause of antibiotic-associated diarrhea and pseudomembranous colitis. The production of toxins A and B leads to inflammation and damage to the intestinal lining, causing symptoms ranging from mild diarrhea to severe, life-threatening colitis.

Diagnosis and Treatment: Accurate identification of C. difficile involves a combination of culture characteristics, toxin detection, and molecular methods. Treatment typically includes discontinuation of the inciting antibiotic, administration of specific antibiotics such as vancomycin or fidaxomicin, and in severe cases, surgical intervention may be necessary.
